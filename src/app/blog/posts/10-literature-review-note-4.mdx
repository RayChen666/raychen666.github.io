## Article One

**[3D Dynamic Scene Graphs: Actionable Spatial Perception with Places, Objects, and Humans](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.06289)**

This is arguably the most foundational paper for our design. Rosinol et al. propose **Dynamic Scene Graphs (DSGs)** — a layered, 
hierarchical graph representation where nodes encode spatial entities (objects, rooms, agents) and edges encode spatio-temporal 
relations between them. The key insight is that the graph is *hierarchical*: it organizes the scene at multiple levels of abstraction 
simultaneously — from individual objects, to places, to rooms, to buildings.

What this means for Multi-Agent-XR is that our current flat adjacency list design (edges between peer objects) is actually 
a **single layer** of what could be a richer multi-level structure. A room-type node sitting above an object cluster is exactly the 
kind of abstraction that makes our memory agent's "semantic layout" step meaningful — we're not just tracking where objects are, 
we're encoding what kind of *zone* they belong to.

The paper also introduces the concept of **actionable representations**: the graph isn't just for visualization or bookkeeping, it 
actively supports planning and decision-making. This maps directly onto how we intend to use our `layout_graph` — the Memory Agent 
extracts it, but the Scene Agent *acts* on it to determine final placement. DSGs formalize this two-stage read/act pattern we've 
been converging toward.

One direct takeaway: our current edge schema (`from`, `to`, `relation`, `side`) is well-aligned with DSG edge semantics. The 
paper's use of "inclusion" and "adjacency" as primary edge types maps onto our `close_to`, `next_to`, and `at_corner` relation 
vocabulary.

## Article Two

**[SQA3D: Situated Question Answering in 3D Scenes](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.07474)**

SQA3D frames 3D scene understanding as a *situated* task: rather than asking "where is the chair?", it asks an agent to first 
understand its own position and orientation in the scene, *then* reason about the surrounding environment to answer questions. The 
dataset covers 6.8k unique situations across 650 ScanNet scenes, with 33k+ questions spanning spatial relation comprehension, 
commonsense reasoning, and multi-hop inference.

The benchmark in the articles argue that 
the best existing models only reach around 47.20% accuracy, while human participants score around 90.06%. This 
performance gap tells us something important — **spatial reasoning in 3D is genuinely hard for current LLMs**, and structured 
representations are critically important, not an optional boost - it affect how AI percepts the world!

For our research framing, SQA3D is useful in two ways. First, it validates that *situatedness* matters — an agent understanding a 
user's natural language command like "put the lamp to the left of the desk" needs to know where it is in the scene, not just the 
global layout. Our Scene Agent implicitly handles this through the current scene JSON, but we haven't formally framed it as a 
situated reasoning problem. Second, the multi-hop reasoning questions in SQA3D mirror the kind of chained inference our Complex 
Route performs: parse intent → retrieve memory layout → check current scene → compute delta → place object. That's at minimum a 
3-hop reasoning chain before any placement happens.

This paper could help us argue in the IEEE VR submission that our pipeline architecture is *designed* to support situated spatial 
reasoning — which is a stronger framing than just "multi-agent system for VR."




## Article Three

**[Visual Genome: Connecting Language and Vision Using Crowdsourced Dense Image Annotations](https://arxiv.org/abs/1602.07332)**

Visual Genome is the dataset infrastructure paper that much of modern scene graph research traces back to. The authors collected 
dense annotations across 100k+ images — objects, attributes, and crucially, **pairwise relationships** between objects expressed 
in natural language. Each image averages 18 pairwise relationships, canonicalized to WordNet synsets, forming a structured 
language-vision bridge.

The relevance to our work is in **relationship vocabulary**. Visual Genome's relationship annotations (e.g., "next to", "on top of", 
"facing", "behind") are exactly the spatial predicates we use in our `layout_graph` edges. The fact that this vocabulary was 
crowdsourced from natural image descriptions — not hand-engineered by researchers — means our chosen relations like `close_to`, 
`next_to`, `facing`, and `adjacent_to` are grounded in how humans *actually* describe spatial scenes in language. That's a 
meaningful point for our paper: our scene graph schema isn't arbitrary, it reflects the canonical predicate set that emerges from 
human spatial language.

More concretely, Visual Genome shows that **relationship triplets** (subject → predicate → object) are the atomic unit of 
spatial language grounding. Our edges are structured identically: `{from: "sofa", to: "back_wall", relation: "next_to", side: "north"}`. 
This alignment with a foundational vision-language dataset strengthens the theoretical basis of our representation choice. 

## Article Four

**[Translating Universal Scene Descriptions into Knowledge Graphs for Robotic Environment](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.16737)**

This is the most directly engineering-adjacent paper of the four. Nguyen et al. investigate virtual reality as a medium for robot 
environment modeling, and propose a pipeline to convert **Universal Scene Description (USD)** format scene graphs into **Knowledge 
Graph (KG)** representations that support semantic querying and integration with external knowledge sources.

**Description** of Universal Scene Description: [OpenUSD](https://openusd.org/release/index.html)

The core idea is that a raw scene graph (geometry, hierarchy, object names) is not the same as an *actionable* knowledge base. By 
translating the scene into a KG — where nodes and edges are typed using ontologies (RDF/OWL) — robots can perform semantic queries 
like "what objects are in the kitchen zone?" or "which objects are on top of a surface-type furniture?" This is essentially what 
our Memory Agent's `layout_graph` is trying to do in lightweight JSON form. 

For Multi-Agent-XR, this paper indicates a **design gap we should be aware of**: our current graph is structural (adjacency + 
relations) but not *semantically typed* — we don't classify objects into ontological categories beyond their names. The paper 
suggests that adding even simple type hierarchies (e.g., `furniture/surface`, `furniture/seating`, `decor/lighting`) would enable 
more principled placement queries. Notably, this echoes the `spatial_relationships.preferred_anchors` fields we've been sketching 
into the extended `metadata.json` — those type-based placement rules are a lightweight ontological layer by another name.
